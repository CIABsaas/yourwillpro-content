<div class="two-column">
  <div class="column-card legal-column">
    <div class="column-label">LEGAL DEFINITION</div>
    <p>The legal and mental competence required to execute a valid will. Generally requires the testator to understand the nature of making a will, the extent of their property, the natural objects of their bounty (family), and how the will distributes their estate. The standard is lower than general contractual capacity.</p>
  </div>
  <div class="column-card plain-column">
    <div class="column-label">PLAIN ENGLISH</div>
    <p>Testamentary capacity means you're mentally capable of making a will. You need to know what you own, who your family is, and what your will does. It's a pretty low bar‚Äîeven people with some mental decline often have testamentary capacity.</p>
  </div>
</div>

<div class="why-matters">
  <h3>üí° Why this matters</h3>
  <p>If you lack testamentary capacity when making a will, it's invalid. But capacity isn't all-or-nothing‚Äîsomeone with dementia or mental illness can still have good days where they understand enough to make a valid will. Getting a medical assessment at the time of signing provides crucial evidence if the will is later challenged.</p>
  <p>This protects both the person making the will and the beneficiaries.</p>
</div>

<div class="did-you-know">
  <h3>üí° Did you know?</h3>
  <p>The test for testamentary capacity was established in 1870 in the famous English case Banks v Goodfellow and is still used in many common law countries today. It's deliberately set at a low threshold to allow people to make wills even if they have some cognitive decline.</p>
