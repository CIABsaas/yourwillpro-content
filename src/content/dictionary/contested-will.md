<div class="two-column">
  <div class="column-card legal-column">
    <div class="column-label">LEGAL DEFINITION</div>
    <p>A will that is subject to legal challenge or dispute through court proceedings. Grounds for contest include lack of testamentary capacity, undue influence, fraud, improper execution, or family provision claims.</p>
  </div>
  <div class="column-card plain-column">
    <div class="column-label">PLAIN ENGLISH</div>
    <p>When someone fights about a will in court. This usually happens when a family member feels they should have inherited more, or believes the will isn't valid because the person who made it lacked mental capacity or was pressured.</p>
  </div>
</div>

<div class="when-box">
  <h3>‚è± When you'll encounter this term</h3>
  <ul>
    <li>A family member was disinherited or received less than expected</li>
    <li>Concerns about the deceased's mental capacity when making the will</li>
    <li>Suspicions of undue influence or fraud</li>
    <li>Claims for greater provision under family law</li>
    <li>Disputes in blended families between spouse and children</li>
  </ul>
</div>

<div class="example-box">
  <div class="example-label">EXAMPLE</div>
  <p>"Dad left everything to his second wife, so my siblings and I are contesting the will because he had dementia when he signed it."</p>
</div>

  <h3>üí° Why this matters</h3>

  <p>Understanding the grounds for contesting helps you either strengthen your own will against challenges, or realistically assess whether a challenge is worth pursuing.</p>
